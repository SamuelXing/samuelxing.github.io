## Paper Reading Self-Evaluation Checklist

For each section, ask yourself:

### 1. Importance — Did I capture *why*, not just *what*?
- [ ] Did I state what the paper **introduced or changed** for the field, not just what it "discusses"?
- [ ] Did I distinguish between "this paper surveys X" vs "this paper *defined the framework* for X"?
- [ ] Would a reader who hasn't read the paper understand why they should care?

### 2. Key Ideas — Did I capture the *argument*, not just the *topics*?
- [ ] Did I name the **concrete contributions** (specific architectures, formulas, algorithms, metrics)?
- [ ] Did I explain the **reasoning/tradeoffs**, not just list topics? ("shared-nothing scales best *because* it avoids resource contention" vs "discussed shared-nothing")
- [ ] Did I write **complete sentences**, not bullet fragments?
- [ ] Is every technical term I used **actually correct**? (Check spelling: parallelism, paradigm, etc.)

### 3. System/Evaluation — Did I add value beyond "no system was built"?
- [ ] If it's a survey/theoretical paper, did I note what **real systems or benchmarks** it references?
- [ ] If a system was built, did I capture **how it was evaluated** and what the **key result** was?

### 4. Judgement — Is this *my* thinking, not a summary of the paper's future work?
- [ ] Did I evaluate from **today's perspective**: what held up, what didn't, what changed?
- [ ] Did I avoid just re-listing the paper's own "future work" section?
- [ ] Did I connect this paper to **other papers I've read** or systems I know?

### 5. Summary (top-of-post paragraph) — Would this stand alone?
- [ ] Does the first paragraph work as a **self-contained summary** without needing to read the details?
- [ ] Is the first sentence the **core contribution**, not a link or metadata?

### Common pitfalls to avoid:
- **Vague verbs**: "discusses", "talks about", "is about" → replace with "defines", "argues", "proves", "proposes"
- **Topic lists instead of arguments**: "Discussed X, Y, and Z" → "Argued X because Y, which enables Z"
- **Echoing instead of judging**: Don't repeat the paper's own conclusions as your judgement — add your own perspective
